# Capability Mapping — Zero-Day Readiness

**Model:** VMMM v2.0.0  
**Domain:** Prepare • **Tier:** Foundational • **Updated:** 2026-01-31

> **Who This Mapping Is For:**
> * Incident response teams coordinating rapid assessment, containment, and remediation when zero-day vulnerabilities or critical threats emerge requiring cross-functional crisis response under extreme time pressure
> * CISOs and security leadership managing crisis communication with executives, boards, customers, and regulators during active exploitation events with compressed decision timelines measured in hours not days
> * IT operations teams executing emergency patches, workarounds, and mitigations on production systems with minimal testing time requiring pre-approved emergency change processes and tested compensating controls
> * Business and executive leadership making risk decisions about service continuity, customer notifications, and public disclosure during active exploitation balancing business impact against security requirements

---

## Executive Summary

Zero-Day Readiness determines how effectively organizations respond when vulnerabilities emerge with active exploitation, no patches available, and compressed decision timelines measured in hours not days. Unlike routine vulnerability management following established remediation SLAs, zero-day response requires crisis coordination across security, IT, business, legal, and communications teams under extreme pressure with incomplete information and escalating consequences. Mature organizations maintain documented crisis playbooks defining response procedures and decision authorities, conduct regular tabletop exercises and simulations discovering gaps before real incidents, establish clear escalation paths and communication templates, and practice coordinated response through realistic drills involving cross-functional teams. Without mature zero-day readiness, organizations discover critical response gaps during actual incidents—unclear ownership of "take offline" decisions, missing stakeholder contact lists, untested compensating controls, decision paralysis while attackers exploit systems, conflicting communications creating stakeholder confusion. This capability strengthens evidence for incident response (IR-4, IR-8) and contingency planning (CP-2) in NIST 800-53, incident management and response communications (RS.MA, RS.CO) in CSF 2.0, and incident response (A.5.26) in ISO 27001. Higher maturity enables organizations to demonstrate crisis response frameworks with real-time threat intelligence integration, workflow automation accelerating assessment and containment, defined escalation paths with documented decision authority at each level, continuous improvement integrating lessons learned from exercises and incidents into updated playbooks, and measured response performance showing organizational resilience as strategic capability.

**Key Frameworks:** NIST 800-53 (IR-4, IR-8, CP-2) • CSF 2.0 (RS.MA, RS.AN, RS.CO) • CIS Control 17 • ISO 27001 (A.5.26, A.5.24)  
**Primary Evidence:** Crisis response playbooks documenting procedures and decision authorities, tabletop exercise records with participant lists and findings, communication templates for stakeholders, decision authority matrix, post-incident reviews with implemented improvements  
**Cross-Domain Dependencies:** Crisis Communication Readiness, Compensating Controls, Threat Intelligence Integration

## Capability Overview

Zero-day vulnerabilities expose the difference between documented processes and organizational muscle memory under pressure. Log4Shell, MOVEit Transfer, SolarWinds Sunburst, EternalBlue—these weren't managed through routine vulnerability management processes following normal SLAs. They hit Friday afternoons requiring emergency executive meetings, forced patch-or-shutdown decisions with incomplete information about business impact, demanded coordination across teams who rarely worked together under normal conditions experiencing first-time collaboration during crisis. Organizations without zero-day readiness discovered critical response gaps in real-time during actual exploitation: nobody knew who makes the "take production offline for emergency patching" decision requiring business approval, communication templates for customer notification didn't exist forcing improvised messaging under regulatory deadlines, compensating controls documented in playbooks were untested discovering implementation failures during deployment, teams burned 12 hours in meetings debating response approach while attackers actively exploited vulnerable systems expanding breach scope.

Without zero-day readiness, systematic failures cascade during crisis response. Sunday morning: CISA issues emergency directive about critical vulnerability in widely deployed VPN appliance with active exploitation observed. Security team discovers organization runs affected appliance providing remote access for 500 employees. Team attempts to contact IT operations for emergency patching. Operations team unavailable—weekend on-call rotation covers infrastructure outages not security incidents, contact information outdated after organizational restructuring. Security escalates to CISO. CISO asks "Can we take VPN offline?" Security team doesn't know—unclear which business processes depend on VPN, no documented business impact analysis exists for remote access infrastructure. CISO attempts to contact CIO for business impact assessment. CIO traveling internationally, deputy CIO responds but lacks authority to approve production service interruption. Hours pass attempting to reach decision makers while vulnerability remains unpatched and exploitable.

Monday morning: Organization decides emergency patching required. IT operations begins change process. Change management board requires 48-hour advance notice for production changes per policy. Security requests emergency change exception. Exception process requires VP approval. VP asks "What's the business impact of taking VPN offline during patch?" Security team doesn't have answer—no documented dependency mapping, no communication plan for affected users. VP asks "What if patch fails?" Operations has no tested rollback procedure for VPN appliance. VP asks "Can we deploy compensating controls instead?" Security team searches playbook finding reference to network segmentation as mitigation. Operations team attempts to implement segmentation discovering firewall rules weren't documented in playbook requiring new rule development consuming additional hours. By Monday afternoon—36 hours after vulnerability disclosure—patch still not deployed, compensating controls partially implemented, organization exposed to active exploitation entire time.

Tuesday: Patch finally deployed. Wednesday: Customer asks "Were we affected by the VPN vulnerability announced Sunday?" Organization realizes no customer notification sent—communications team never engaged, no template existed for vulnerability disclosure, legal review of notification language required consuming days. Regulatory inquiry arrives Thursday asking why notification delayed beyond 72-hour requirement. Post-incident review reveals systematic gaps: no designated incident commander for zero-day events, decision authority unclear at every escalation point, communication templates nonexistent requiring creation during crisis, compensating controls documented but never tested discovering deployment failures under pressure, business impact analysis missing preventing rapid decision making.

Mature Zero-Day Readiness means practicing crisis before crisis arrives preventing discovery of gaps during actual incidents. Crisis playbook documents: Who gets called when zero-day announced? (Incident commander, security lead, IT operations lead, business owner, communications lead—with primary and backup contacts, mobile numbers, after-hours availability). Who has decision authority for service interruption? (Documented in decision matrix: CISO authorized up to 4-hour outage, CIO required for 4-24 hours, CEO required beyond 24 hours). What communication templates exist for different stakeholder groups? (Customers, regulators, media, employees, board—with pre-approved language frameworks requiring only incident-specific details). What compensating controls can be deployed quickly? (Documented with deployment procedures, tested quarterly through exercises, automation available for common scenarios). Where's the emergency change process? (Pre-approved emergency change template, designated approvers with after-hours contact, expedited review process documented).

Teams practice through tabletop exercises and simulations discovering gaps in safe environments: simulation reveals VPN decision maker on vacation, backup not designated—process updated. Exercise discovers firewall team doesn't have authority to implement segmentation without change approval—pre-approved emergency rule set created. Drill identifies communications template references wrong legal entity after acquisition—templates updated. Decision trees pre-define escalation criteria: At what exploitation threshold do we notify customers? (Active exploitation observed affecting customer data triggers immediate notification). When do we escalate to CEO/board? (Potential for business interruption exceeding 24 hours or regulatory penalties exceeding $1M triggers executive briefing). What constitutes "take offline" criteria? (Documented decision framework: active exploitation + no patch available + no effective compensating controls = offline decision authorized).

Crisis communication templates exist for different audiences: Executive briefing template summarizes technical impact in business terms with decision options and risk tradeoff analysis. Customer notification template explains incident scope, customer impact, remediation timeline, contact information for questions. Regulatory notification template addresses specific compliance requirements with required disclosure elements. Media statement template provides approved messaging preventing conflicting public communications. Post-incident reviews capture lessons learned: every exercise produces findings report with assigned improvement actions, actual incidents undergo root cause analysis identifying process gaps, improvements integrated into next exercise validating effectiveness.

When real zero-day hits, mature organizations activate practiced playbooks rather than improvising under pressure. Sunday CISA directive received. Security team consults playbook identifying incident commander (security director with designated backup). Commander activates crisis team via pre-configured communication channel (dedicated Slack channel, conference bridge pre-scheduled for zero-day events). Team includes security lead (assessment and remediation), IT operations lead (implementation), business owner (impact decisions), communications lead (stakeholder notification). Assessment completed within 2 hours using documented procedure: affected systems identified from configuration management database, business impact determined from service dependency mapping, compensating controls evaluated from tested playbook. Decision matrix consulted: VPN outage 4 hours estimated, CISO authorized, approval obtained. Emergency change initiated using pre-approved template, operations executes patch using tested runbook, compensating controls deployed automatically via tested scripts. Customer notification sent using pre-approved template within 24 hours. Regulatory notification submitted within 72 hours meeting compliance deadline. Response time measured: vulnerability announcement to patch deployment 8 hours instead of 36 hours from previous incident.

At highest maturity, zero-day readiness operates as continuous capability improvement cycle. Post-incident review completed within week capturing timeline, decision points, effectiveness measures, improvement opportunities. Findings: patch deployment time exceeded target by 2 hours due to unforeseen compatibility issue, rollback testing should be enhanced, automation deployed compensating controls successfully reducing manual effort. Improvements implemented: enhanced compatibility testing added to patch validation, automated rollback procedures developed and tested, additional compensating control scenarios automated. Metrics tracked showing improvement over time: average time to patch deployment decreased from 36 hours to 8 hours to 6 hours across last three zero-day events, customer notification time improved from 72 hours to 24 hours to 12 hours, decision-making cycle time reduced from multiple meetings to designated authority approval. Strategic value demonstrated: board briefed on zero-day response capability as competitive differentiator, investor relations uses demonstrated crisis management as risk mitigation evidence, customer contracts reference mature incident response capability.

## Maturity-Based Evidence Progression

| Maturity Level | Evidence Characteristics |
|----------------|-------------------------|
| **Level 1** | No formal zero-day response process exists. Teams react inconsistently when critical vulnerabilities emerge. Limited coordination between security, IT, and business teams. No documentation of who should do what during crisis. Response depends entirely on individual initiative and tribal knowledge. Evidence: None—no playbooks, templates, or documented procedures exist. Email threads and chat logs show uncoordinated ad hoc response with confusion about roles and authorities. |
| **Level 2** | Ad hoc procedures exist but response depends on individual heroics and institutional memory. Some teams maintain informal "call these people" contact lists and basic runbooks. Response not repeatable—different people follow different approaches each incident. Ownership unclear during crises leading to escalation delays. Post-incident reviews happen sometimes but improvements rarely systematically implemented. Evidence: Personal notes in wiki or shared drives, informal contact lists in spreadsheets, scattered email threads showing uncoordinated response, inconsistent documentation quality across different incidents, improvement recommendations without implementation tracking. |
| **Level 3** | Defined crisis response process documented in playbook accessible to all teams: communication channels established (dedicated Slack/Teams channel, conference bridge, incident tracking system), designated incident leads assigned with primary and backup contacts, response steps documented from detection through resolution. Roles and responsibilities clearly defined (who assesses technical impact, who decides service interruption, who implements remediation, who communicates to stakeholders). Emergency change process defined with pre-approved templates and designated approvers. Basic playbook exists covering standard workflow: detection, assessment, containment, remediation, communication. Evidence: Documented crisis response plan with version control and review dates, assigned roles with contact information, communication templates for common scenarios, emergency change procedure with approval authorities, evidence of plan socialization (training records, acknowledgment forms) to relevant teams. |
| **Level 4** | Crisis readiness supported by detailed playbooks covering various scenarios (internet-facing vulnerability vs. internal, active exploitation observed vs. theoretical risk, patch available vs. mitigation-only scenarios). Pre-established workflows integrate with ticketing systems, change management platforms, communication tools enabling automation. Regular crisis simulations conducted (quarterly tabletop exercises minimum, annual full-scale drills) involving cross-functional teams from security, IT, business, legal, communications. Response timelines measured (time-to-assessment, time-to-containment, time-to-patch, time-to-notification) with improvement targets. Lessons learned from exercises and actual incidents systematically tracked with implementation verification. Evidence: Scenario-specific playbooks with decision trees and escalation paths, simulation exercise records including participant lists, scenario details, findings reports with improvement actions, response timeline metrics dashboards, documented improvements from lessons learned with before/after comparison, integration configurations showing automated workflows between security tools and ticketing/communication systems. |
| **Level 5** | Cross-functional crisis response framework operates as continuous capability with real-time threat intelligence feeds integrated into automated detection and assessment systems. Workflow automation accelerates response: automated asset identification (scanning for affected software versions), pre-approved emergency changes (automatic deployment of tested compensating controls), integration with communication platforms (automated stakeholder notification drafting). Defined escalation paths with documented decision authority at each level (operational, tactical, strategic) mapped to impact severity and business risk. Continuous improvement embedded: every exercise and incident generates lessons learned automatically entered into knowledge base, playbooks updated through change management with stakeholder review, response metrics tracked in dashboard showing improvement trends over time, retrospective analysis identifies systemic issues beyond individual incidents. Strategic integration: zero-day response performance discussed in risk committee meetings, capability maturity informs cyber insurance underwriting, response metrics included in executive KPI dashboards, demonstrated capability referenced in customer security questionnaires and RFP responses. Evidence: Automated threat intelligence integration configurations, response automation scripts with testing records, metrics dashboards showing response time improvements and trends, lessons learned database with search functionality, playbook version history showing continuous updates, executive briefing materials presenting zero-day readiness as strategic capability, cyber insurance premium reductions citing mature incident response, customer references citing response capability. |

## Maturity Investment Guidance

**Foundational Investment (Levels 1–2):**  
Document basic zero-day crisis response playbook defining: who gets called when zero-day announced (incident commander with backup contact, security lead, IT operations lead, business owner with authority to approve service interruption, communications lead for stakeholder notification—all with after-hours contact information and escalation procedures), what initial assessment includes (affected systems identification, exploitation status determination, patch availability check, compensating control evaluation), what decision criteria apply (when to escalate, when to interrupt service, when to notify customers), what communication templates exist (internal notification, executive briefing, customer notification basic framework). Designate incident commander for zero-day events with documented authority and backup. Create contact list with primary and backup contacts for all key roles. Define basic emergency change process allowing expedited patching when zero-day exploitation observed. Conduct initial tabletop exercise walking through playbook with key stakeholders identifying obvious gaps and improving documentation. **ROI:** Eliminates confusion about "who does what" during first critical hours of zero-day response when rapid coordination essential, provides decision authorities preventing escalation delays and consensus paralysis consuming hours while exploitation continues, establishes communication framework preventing improvised messaging creating stakeholder confusion and potential regulatory violations, identifies critical gaps through exercise before discovering during actual crisis when stakes highest.

**Enhanced Investment (Levels 3–4):**  
Develop detailed scenario-based playbooks covering different zero-day types: internet-facing vulnerability requiring immediate action, internal vulnerability allowing more deliberate response, active exploitation observed requiring emergency measures, theoretical vulnerability allowing testing before deployment, patch available enabling rapid remediation, mitigation-only requiring compensating controls implementation. Integrate zero-day response with existing systems: incident tracking (automated ticket creation when zero-day detected), change management (pre-approved emergency change template auto-populating affected systems), communication platforms (stakeholder notification templates integrated with email/SMS systems). Establish regular exercise program: quarterly tabletop exercises rotating scenarios and participants, annual full-scale simulation including after-hours activation and actual system changes in test environment, post-exercise improvement tracking with verification before next exercise. Implement response metrics: time-to-assessment (from detection to impact analysis complete), time-to-containment (from detection to initial protective measures deployed), time-to-patch (from patch availability to deployment complete), time-to-notification (from exploitation confirmation to stakeholder notification sent). Establish lessons learned process: every exercise produces findings report with assigned improvement owners and deadlines, actual incidents undergo formal root cause analysis, improvement implementation verified through follow-up exercises. **ROI:** Scenario-specific playbooks reduce decision time by providing pre-analyzed options for common situations, automated workflow integration eliminates manual coordination delays cutting hours from response timeline, regular exercises build organizational muscle memory enabling teams to execute under pressure without improvisation, metrics enable continuous improvement showing response capability trending toward targets, systematic lessons learned prevent recurring issues improving effectiveness over time.

**Strategic Investment (Level 5):**  
Implement automated threat intelligence integration: real-time feeds from vulnerability databases, threat intelligence platforms, and vendor security bulletins automatically parsed identifying relevant zero-days based on asset inventory matching, automated asset scanning triggered when zero-day announced identifying affected systems within minutes not hours, automated compensating control deployment for common scenarios (network segmentation rules, application firewall policies, monitoring rule updates) tested quarterly and auto-deployed when specific zero-day types detected. Establish comprehensive response automation: pre-approved emergency changes automatically submitted when zero-day severity and exploitation status meet defined thresholds, automated stakeholder notification drafting pulling details from assessment systems and populating templates with incident-specific information, workflow orchestration automatically routing assessment, approval, implementation, and communication tasks to appropriate teams based on scenario type and impact level. Develop advanced decision support: decision trees with quantified risk/business impact tradeoffs presented to decision makers, automated business impact analysis pulling service dependency mapping and calculating outage costs, scenario simulation capability modeling different response options showing projected outcomes. Implement continuous capability measurement: response performance dashboards tracking metrics trends across incidents and exercises, capability maturity assessment comparing performance against industry benchmarks, strategic planning integration using zero-day readiness metrics in risk discussions and investment decisions. Establish strategic capability demonstration: zero-day response capability featured in customer security reviews demonstrating resilience, response metrics shared with cyber insurance providers supporting premium negotiations, capability maturity communicated to board showing organizational resilience as competitive differentiator, response performance referenced in incident disclosure showing effective crisis management. **ROI:** Automation reduces response timeline from hours to minutes for initial assessment and deployment of tested controls dramatically reducing exposure window, automated workflows eliminate coordination delays and human errors improving response consistency and effectiveness, advanced decision support enables better risk decisions under pressure balancing security protection against business continuity, continuous measurement demonstrates improving capability supporting investment justification and strategic positioning, demonstrated capability provides competitive advantage in customer acquisition and cyber insurance premium reduction.

## Practical Applications

This capability mapping may be used to:

- **Crisis Playbook Development:** Create documented response procedures for zero-day events defining roles, authorities, communication channels, assessment criteria, decision workflows, and escalation paths ensuring teams can coordinate effectively under pressure
- **Tabletop Exercise Design:** Develop realistic zero-day scenarios for quarterly exercises testing coordination between security, IT, business, legal, and communications teams discovering gaps in procedures, authorities, and capabilities before actual crisis events
- **Decision Authority Framework:** Establish documented matrix defining who has authority to make critical decisions (service interruption, emergency patching, customer notification, regulatory disclosure) at different impact levels preventing delays during high-pressure response
- **Communication Template Creation:** Develop pre-approved message frameworks for different stakeholder audiences (executives, customers, regulators, media, employees) enabling rapid consistent communications during crisis without improvising messaging under pressure requiring legal review during incident
- **Response Metrics Program:** Implement measurement framework tracking time-to-assessment, time-to-containment, time-to-patch, and time-to-notification across exercises and incidents enabling continuous improvement and demonstrating capability maturity trend to leadership
- **Audit Preparation:** Demonstrate incident response capability maturity to auditors through documented playbooks, exercise records, metrics showing improvement, and post-incident reviews with implemented improvements proving organizational crisis readiness

## Boundaries & Limitations

**What This Mapping Is NOT:**
- ❌ A guarantee that zero-day response will always be successful (preparation reduces chaos and improves outcomes but doesn't eliminate inherent risk of previously unknown vulnerabilities)
- ❌ A complete incident response playbook ready for use without customization (organizations must create playbooks reflecting their specific environment, systems, business processes, and risk tolerance)
- ❌ A replacement for threat intelligence and ongoing vulnerability management capabilities (detection and assessment of zero-days require other organizational capabilities providing inputs to crisis response)
- ❌ A claim that any specific response process automatically satisfies all framework requirements (effectiveness must be demonstrated through testing, documentation, and evidence of improvement over time)

**Critical Dependencies:**
- Zero-Day Readiness depends on Threat Intelligence Integration (provides zero-day detection and technical assessment), Compensating Controls (provides emergency mitigation options when patches unavailable), Crisis Communication Readiness (provides stakeholder notification procedures and templates), and Program Governance (provides executive decision authority and approval processes for service interruption). Without these capabilities, playbooks lack necessary inputs about threats, available protective measures, communication channels, and decision-making authority required for effective crisis response.

**Common Misinterpretation:**
- Organizations often confuse "having a documented playbook" with actual readiness capability. Mature capability requires regular practice through realistic exercises—playbooks that sit on shelf untested under pressure often fail when needed because procedures prove unworkable, contact information becomes outdated, authorities change, and teams lack muscle memory for coordination under stress. Additionally, zero-day readiness isn't one-time achievement requiring only initial documentation—as systems evolve, personnel turnover occurs, business processes change, and threat landscape shifts, readiness degrades without continuous exercise program identifying gaps and continuous improvement updating playbooks to reflect current environment.

**Important Notes:**
- Zero-day response often requires critical decisions with incomplete information—effective playbooks include decision criteria and risk acceptance thresholds guiding judgment calls, not just technical procedures assuming perfect knowledge
- Cross-functional coordination between security, IT, business, legal, and communications teams is critical success factor—security alone cannot respond effectively to zero-days affecting production systems requiring business continuity decisions and stakeholder communications
- Legal and communications considerations often overlooked in technical playbooks—customer notification requirements, regulatory reporting deadlines, and public disclosure decisions require advance planning with pre-approved frameworks reviewed by legal counsel
- Response performance metrics valuable for improvement but can create perverse incentives if measuring speed without considering decision quality—fast bad decision worse than slightly slower good decision requiring appropriate balance
